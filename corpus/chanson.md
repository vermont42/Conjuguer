# La Chanson de Roland — full-treatment edition

*Oxford manuscript (Bodleian Digby 23), canonical Bédier line numbering (1–4002,
291 laisses).* Each laisse is a self-contained block: the **numbered original** Old
French (with the **modern infinitive of every verb** bracketed at the end of each
line), immediately followed by a **fresh line-by-line translation** sharing the same
canonical numbers. See `docs/chanson-full-treatment-prompt.md` for the conventions and
the verb-gloss mappings table.

**Translation note.** The translation below is my own (Claude's), re-rendered line for
line. It is faithful over poetic; lines I am unsure of carry a trailing
`<!-- uncertain: ... -->` comment rather than a confident guess.

**Edition note.** Laisse I was transcribed from the UT-Austin Bédier text (`amet`,
`Saraguce`, `emperere`). From Laisse II onward the original is transcribed verbatim
from the working raw source `corpus/chanson-roland-oxford.txt` (Orbis Latinus /
orbilat.com), which differs in minor orthography and punctuation (`aimet`,
`Sarraguce`, `emper[er]e`, editorial brackets like `[S]erai`, parentheticals like
`(...)`). Line numbers and laisse divisions agree across both.

## Laisse I (lines 1–9)

**Original**
1. Carles li reis, nostre emperere magnes
2. set anz tuz pleins ad estet en Espaigne: [avoir, être]
3. Tresqu'en la mer cunquist la tere altaigne. [conquérir]
4. N'i ad castel ki devant lui remaigne; [avoir, remaindre (rester)]
5. Mur ne citet n'i est remés a fraindre [être, remaindre (rester), fraindre (briser)]
6. Fors Saraguce, ki est en une muntaigne. [être]
7. Li reis Marsilie la tient, ki Deu nen amet, [tenir, aimer]
8. Mahumet sert e Apollin recleimet: [servir, réclamer]
9. Nes poet guarder que mals ne l'i ateignet. AOI. [pouvoir, garder, atteindre]

**Translation**
1. Charles the king, our great emperor,
2. has been in Spain seven full years:
3. he conquered the high land all the way to the sea.
4. There is no fortress that stands against him;
5. no wall or city is left to be breached
6. except Saragossa, which sits upon a mountain.
7. King Marsile holds it — he who does not love God,
8. serves Mahomet and calls upon Apollin;
9. he cannot keep harm from reaching him there. AOI.

## Laisse II (lines 10–23)

**Original**
10. Li reis Marsilie esteit en Sarraguce. [être]
11. Alez en est en un verger suz l'umbre; [aller, être]
12. Sur un perrun de marbre bloi se culchet, [se coucher]
13. Envirun lui plus de vint milie humes.
14. Il en apelet e ses dux e ses cuntes: [appeler]
15. «Oëz, seignurs, quel pecchet nus encumbret: [ouïr (entendre), encombrer (accabler)]
16. Li emper[er]es Carles de France dulce
17. En cest païs nos est venuz cunfundre. [être, venir, confondre]
18. Jo nen ai ost qui bataille li dunne, [avoir, donner]
19. Ne n'ai tel gent ki la sue derumpet. [avoir, derumpre (rompre)]
20. Cunseilez mei cume mi savie hume, [conseiller]
21. Si m(e) guarisez e de mort et de hunte.» [garir (guérir)] <!-- uncertain: guarir here = 'protéger/sauver' rather than modern 'guérir' -->
22. N'i ad paien ki un sul mot respundet, [avoir, répondre]
23. Fors Blancandrins de Castel de Valfunde.

**Translation**
10. King Marsile was in Saragossa.
11. He has gone off into an orchard beneath the shade;
12. Upon a block of blue-grey marble he lies down,
13. Around him more than twenty thousand men.
14. He calls forth both his dukes and his counts:
15. «Hear, my lords, what calamity weighs upon us:
16. Charlemagne the emperor of sweet France
17. Has come into this land to destroy us.
18. I have no army that may give him battle,
19. Nor have I such a host as might break his.
20. Counsel me, as my wise men,
21. And save me from death and from shame.»
22. There is no pagan who answers a single word,
23. Except Blancandrin from the Castle of Valfunde.

## Laisse III (lines 24–46)

**Original**
24. Blancandrins fut des plus saives paiens: [être]
25. De vasselage fut asez chevaler, [être]
26. Prozdom i out pur sun seignur aider; [avoir, aider]
27. E dist al rei: «Ore ne vus esmaiez! [dire, esmaier (s'inquiéter)]
28. Mandez Carlun a l'orguillus, (e) al fier, [mander]
29. Fedeilz servises e mult granz amistez.
30. Vos li durrez urs e leons e chens, [donner]
31. Set cenz camelz e mil hosturs muers,
32. D'or e d'argent .IIII.C. muls cargez, [charger]
33. Cinquante carre, qu'en ferat carier: [faire, carier (charrier)]
34. Ben en purrat luer ses soldeiers. [pouvoir, louer]
35. En ceste tere ad asez osteiet; [avoir, osteier (guerroyer)]
36. En France, ad Ais, s'en deit ben repairer. [devoir, repairer (rentrer)]
37. Vos le sivrez a la feste seint Michel: [suivre]
38. Si recevrez la lei de chrestiens, [recevoir]
39. Serez ses hom par honur e par ben. [être]
40. S'en volt ostages, e vos l'en enveiez, [vouloir, envoyer]
41. U dis u vint pur lui afiancer. [afiancer (garantir)]
42. Enveiu[n]s i les filz de noz muillers: [envoyer]
43. Par nun d'ocire i enveierai le men. [ocire (tuer), envoyer]
44. Asez est melz qu'il i perdent le chefs, [être, perdre]
45. Que nus perduns l'onur ne la deintet, [perdre]
46. Ne nus seiuns cunduiz a mendeier.» AOI. [être, conduire, mendier]

**Translation**
24. Blancandrin was among the wisest pagans:
25. In valor he was very much a knight,
26. A worthy man, and there he was to aid his lord;
27. And he said to the king: «Now do not be dismayed!
28. Send word to Charles, to the proud, to the fierce,
29. Of loyal services and very great friendship.
30. You will give him bears and lions and dogs,
31. Seven hundred camels and a thousand moulted hawks,
32. Four hundred mules laden with gold and silver,
33. Fifty carts, that he will have carried away: <!-- uncertain: "qu'en ferat carier" - "which he will have carted/hauled" -->
34. Well will he be able to pay his soldiers with it.
35. In this land he has campaigned long enough;
36. To France, to Aix, he ought well to return.
37. You will follow him there at the feast of Saint Michael:
38. Thus you will receive the law of the Christians,
39. You will be his man in honor and in good faith.
40. If he wants hostages, then send them to him,
41. Either ten or twenty, to give him assurance.
42. Let us send there the sons of our wives:
43. Even at the risk of death I will send mine. <!-- uncertain: "Par nun d'ocire" - lit. "in the name of killing," i.e. though it mean death -->
44. It is far better that they lose their heads,
45. Than that we lose our honor and our dignity,
46. And that we be reduced to begging.» AOI.

## Laisse IV (lines 47–61)

**Original**
47. Dist Blancandrins: «Pa[r] ceste meie destre [dire]
48. E par la barbe ki al piz me ventelet, [venteler (flotter)]
49. L'ost des Franceis verrez sempres desfere. [voir, desfaire (défaire)]
50. Francs s'en irunt en France la lur tere. [aller]
51. Quant cascuns ert a sun meillor repaire, [être]
52. Carles serat ad Ais, a sa capele; [être]
53. A seint Michel tendrat mult halte feste. [tenir]
54. Vendrat li jurz, si passerat li termes, [venir, passer]
55. N'orrat de nos paroles ne nuveles. [oïr (entendre)]
56. Li reis est fiers e sis curages pesmes: [être]
57. De noz ostages ferat tre[n]cher les testes; [faire, trancher]
58. Asez est mielz, qu'il i perdent les testes, [être, perdre]
59. Que nus perduns clere Espaigne, la bele, [perdre]
60. Ne nus aiuns les mals ne les suffraites.» [avoir]
61. Dient paien: «Issi poet il ben estre!» [dire, pouvoir, être]

**Translation**
47. Blancandrin said: «By this my right hand
48. And by the beard that flutters on my chest,
49. You will soon see the host of the Franks disband.
50. The Franks will go away to France, their own land.
51. When each is back at his best dwelling,
52. Charles will be at Aix, in his chapel;
53. At Saint Michael's he will hold a very high feast.
54. The day will come, and so the term will pass,
55. He will hear of us neither word nor news.
56. The king is fierce and his heart is cruel:
57. He will have the heads of our hostages cut off;
58. It is far better that they lose their heads there,
59. Than that we lose bright Spain, the fair,
60. And that we have the woes and the privations.»
61. The pagans say: «It may well be so!»

## Laisse V (lines 62–77)

62. Li reis Marsilie out sun cunseill finet: [avoir, finer (finir)]
63. Sin apelat Clarin (...) de Balaguet, [appeler]
64. Estamarin e Eudropin, sun per,
65. E Priamun e Guarlan le barbet,
66. E Machiner e sun uncle, Maheu,
67. E Joüner e Malbien d'ultremer,
68. E Blancandrins, por la raisun cunter. [conter]

70. «Seignurs baruns, a Carlemagnes irez; [aller]
71. Il est al siege a Cordres la citet. [être]
72. Branches d'olives en voz mains porterez, [porter]
73. Ço senefiet pais e humilitet. [senefier (signifier)]
74. Par voz saveirs sem puez acorder, [savoir, pouvoir, acorder (accorder)]
75. Jo vos durrai or e argent asez, [donner]
76. Teres e fiéz tant cum vos en vuldrez.» [vouloir]
77. Dient paien: «De ço avun nus asez!» AOI. [dire, avoir]

**Translation**
62. King Marsile had ended his council:
63. So he summoned Clarin (...) of Balaguer,
64. Estamarin and Eudropin, his peer,
65. And Priamon and Guarlan the bearded,
66. And Machiner and his uncle, Maheu,
67. And Jouner and Malbien from overseas,
68. And Blancandrin, to set forth the matter.
69. Of the most felonious he has summoned ten of them:
70. «Lord barons, you shall go to Charlemagne;
71. He is at the siege of the city of Cordres.
72. Olive branches you shall carry in your hands,
73. This signifies peace and humility.
74. By your wisdom, if you can bring me to an accord,
75. I shall give you gold and silver in plenty,
76. Lands and fiefs as much as you will want of them.»
77. The pagans say: «Of this we have enough!» AOI.

## Laisse VI (lines 78–88)

**Original**
78. Li reis Marsilie out finet sun cunseill; [avoir, finer (finir)]
79. Dist a ses humes: «Seignurs, vos en ireiz; [dire, aller]
80. Branches d'olive en voz mains portereiz, [porter]
81. Si me direz a Carlemagne, le rei, [dire]
82. Pur le soen Deu qu'il ait m(er)ercit de mei. [avoir]
83. Ja einz ne verrat passer cest premer meis, [voir, passer]
84. Que jel sivrai od mil de mes fedeilz, [sivre (suivre)]
85. Si recevrai la chrestiene lei, [recevoir]
86. [S]erai ses hom par amur e par feid; [être]
87. S'il voelt ostages, il en avrat par veir.» [voloir (vouloir), avoir]
88. Dist Blancandrins: «Mult bon plait en avreiz.» AOI. [dire, avoir]

**Translation**
78. King Marsile had ended his council;
79. He said to his men: «Lords, you shall go forth;
80. Olive branches you shall carry in your hands,
81. And you shall say for me to Charlemagne, the king,
82. For the sake of his God, that he have mercy on me.
83. He will not see this first month pass
84. Before I follow him with a thousand of my faithful,
85. And I will receive the Christian faith,
86. I will be his man through love and through faith;
87. If he wants hostages, he shall have them indeed.»
88. Blancandrin said: «You shall have a very good agreement out of it.» AOI.

## Laisse VIII (lines 96–121)

**Original**

97. Cordres ad prise e les murs peceiez, [avoir, prendre, peceier (dépecer)]

107. E si i furent e gerin et gerers, [être]
108. La u cist furent, des altres i out bien: [être, avoir]

**Translation**
96. The emperor makes himself both joyful and glad,

98. With his siege engines he beats down its towers.
99. His knights take very great booty from it

101. In the city there remains no pagan,
102. Who is not slain, or becomes a Christian.
103. The emperor is in a great orchard,
104. Together with him Roland and Oliver,
105. Duke Samson and Anseïs the fierce,
106. Geoffrey of Anjou, the king's standard-bearer,
107. And there too were Gerin and Gerier,
108. Where these were, there were indeed many others:
109. From sweet France there are fifteen thousand.
110. Upon white silks those knights are seated,
111. At backgammon they play to amuse themselves
112. And at chess the wisest and the old,
113. And the nimble young squires fence.
114. Beneath a pine, beside an eglantine,
115. A throne they have made all of pure gold,
116. There sits the king who holds sweet France.
117. White is his beard and all blossomed his head,
118. Noble is his body and his bearing proud,
119. He is so, whoever seeks him, that there is no need to point him out.
120. And the messengers dismounted on foot,
121. And greeted him with love and good will.
