# La Chanson de Roland — Laisses I and VIII

Original Old French text (laisse I, lines 1–9; laisse VIII, lines 96–121),
transcribed from the *Old French Online* lesson by Brigitte L.M. Bauer and
Jonathan Slocum, Linguistics Research Center, University of Texas at Austin
(<https://lrc.la.utexas.edu/eieol/ofrol/10>).

Line numbers follow the canonical numbering of the poem. At the end of each
original line, the **modern infinitive forms** of any verbs in that line are
given in brackets. Where the Old French verb has no surviving modern reflex (or
the reflex has drifted in meaning), the bracket shows the Old French infinitive
followed by the nearest current modern infinitive in parentheses — e.g.
`remaindre (rester)`, `estovoir (falloir)`, `ocire (tuer)`. Lines with no verb
carry no bracket.

The translation that follows is my own. It takes inspiration from the lesson's
crib and glossary but was re-rendered line for line.

## Original (Old French)

1. Carles li reis, nostre emperere magnes
2. set anz tuz pleins ad estet en Espaigne: [avoir, être]
3. Tresqu'en la mer cunquist la tere altaigne. [conquérir]
4. N'i ad castel ki devant lui remaigne; [avoir, remaindre (rester)]
5. Mur ne citet n'i est remés a fraindre [être, remaindre (rester), fraindre (briser)]
6. Fors Saraguce, ki est en une muntaigne. [être]
7. Li reis Marsilie la tient, ki Deu nen amet, [tenir, aimer]
8. Mahumet sert e Apollin recleimet: [servir, réclamer]
9. Nes poet guarder que mals ne l'i ateignet. AOI. [pouvoir, garder, atteindre]

96. Li empereres se fait e balz e liez: [faire]
97. Cordres ad prise e les murs peceiez, [avoir, prendre, peceier (dépecer)]
98. Od ses cadables les turs en abatied; [abattre]
99. Mult grant eschech en unt si chevaler [avoir]
100. D'or e d'argent e de guarnemenz chers.
101. En la citet nen ad remés paien [avoir, remaindre (rester)]
102. Ne seit ocis u devient chrestien. [être, ocire (tuer), devenir]
103. Li empereres est en un grant verger, [être]
104. Ensembl'od lui Rollant e Oliver,
105. Sansun li dux e Anseis li fiers,
106. Gefreid d'Anjou, le rei gunfanuner,
107. E si i furent e Gerin e Gerers; [être]
108. La u cist furent, des altres i out bien: [être, avoir]
109. De dulce France i ad quinze milliers. [avoir]
110. Sur palies blancs siedent cil cevaler, [seoir (asseoir)]
111. As tables juent pur els esbaneier [jouer, esbaneier (s'amuser)]
112. E as eschecs li plus saive e li veill,
113. E escremissent cil bacheler leger. [escrimer]
114. Desuz un pin, delez un eglentier,
115. Un faldestoed i unt, fait tut d'or mer: [avoir, faire]
116. La siet li reis ki dulce France tient. [seoir (asseoir), tenir]
117. Blanche ad la barbe e tut flurit le chef, [avoir, fleurir]
118. Gent ad le cors e le cuntenant fier: [avoir]
119. S'est kil demandet, ne l'estoet enseigner. [être, demander, estovoir (falloir), enseigner]
120. E li message descendirent a pied, [descendre]
121. Sil saluerent par amur e par bien. [saluer]

## Translation (my own)

1. Charles the king, our great emperor,
2. has been in Spain seven full years:
3. he conquered the high land all the way to the sea.
4. There is no fortress that stands against him;
5. no wall or city is left to be breached
6. except Saragossa, which sits upon a mountain.
7. King Marsile holds it — he who does not love God,
8. serves Mahomet and calls upon Apollin;
9. he cannot keep harm from reaching him there. AOI.

96. The emperor makes himself both merry and glad:
97. he has taken Cordres and smashed its walls to pieces,
98. with his siege-engines he casts down its towers;
99. great is the plunder his knights have won —
100. gold and silver and costly war-gear.
101. In the city no pagan has remained
102. who is not slain or turned Christian.
103. The emperor is in a great orchard,
104. and with him are Roland and Oliver,
105. Samson the duke and Anseis the fierce,
106. Geoffrey of Anjou, the king's standard-bearer,
107. and there too were Gerin and Gerier;
108. where these men were, there were many others besides:
109. of sweet France there are fifteen thousand.
110. Upon white silken cloths those knights are seated,
111. at the gaming-tables they play to amuse themselves,
112. and at chess the wisest and the aged,
113. while the nimble young squires fence.
114. Beneath a pine, beside a wild rose,
115. they have a throne, made all of pure gold:
116. there sits the king who holds sweet France.
117. White is his beard and his head all blossomed gray,
118. fair is his body and proud his bearing:
119. should anyone ask for him, there is no need to point him out.
120. And the messengers dismounted on foot,
121. and greeted him in love and in good will.
